Price elasticity, cross-price effects, expected utility, and risk premium — interactive tools for economics.

Price elasticity of demand (ε) measures how responsive quantity demanded is to a change in price. It answers: if price rises by 1%, how much does quantity fall?

The formula is: ε = (∂q/∂p) × (p/q). Because demand slopes downward, ε is always negative for demand.

Elastic demand (|ε| > 1): quantity is very sensitive to price. A price increase causes revenue to fall because the quantity drop outweighs the price gain.

Inelastic demand (|ε| < 1): quantity is not very sensitive to price. A price increase causes revenue to rise because the price gain outweighs the quantity drop.

Unit elastic (|ε| = 1): revenue is unchanged for a small price change.

Cross-price elasticity measures how the quantity demanded of Good i responds to a change in the price of Good j.

Substitutes (ε > 0): when the price of Good j rises, consumers buy more of Good i instead. Example: Pepsi and Coke.

Complements (ε < 0): when the price of Good j rises, consumers buy less of Good i too. Example: cars and gasoline.

Independent (ε ≈ 0): the price of Good j has no meaningful effect on demand for Good i.

Expected utility is not the same as expected payoff. When consumers are risk-averse, they have diminishing marginal utility of wealth — each additional dollar is worth less than the last.

Expected Payoff = Σ (probability × payoff). This is just the probability-weighted average outcome.

Expected Utility = Σ (probability × u(payoff)). This applies the utility function first, then takes the weighted average.

Because of diminishing marginal utility, a risk-averse person prefers a certain $1M over a 50/50 shot at $2M — even though both have the same expected payoff. The utility function captures this preference.

The utility function you choose determines how risk-averse the agent is: √x and ln(x) are concave (risk-averse), x is linear (risk-neutral), and x² is convex (risk-seeking).

Risk premium is how much of the expected payoff you would give up to eliminate all uncertainty. It measures the cost of risk to a risk-averse agent.

Certainty Equivalent (CE) is the guaranteed amount that gives you the same utility as the risky investment. If CE = $80K and the expected payoff is $100K, you'd accept $80K for certain rather than take the gamble.

Risk Premium = Expected Payoff − Certainty Equivalent. In the example above, the risk premium is $20K — that's how much you'd pay to eliminate the risk.

Willingness to Pay (WTP) is the maximum you'd pay today for the risky asset, equal to the present value of the certainty equivalent (ignoring discount rates here, WTP = CE).

The larger the risk premium, the more risk-averse the agent. A risk-neutral agent has a risk premium of zero.
