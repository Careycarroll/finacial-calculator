🏗️ Cost Structure & Allocation

Compare fixed vs variable cost structures, allocate overhead, classify costs, and avoid the unit cost trap.

Cost structure is the mix of fixed costs (FC) and variable costs (VC). Two firms can have the same price but very different risk/return profiles.

Breakeven units increase when fixed costs are higher (holding price and VC/unit constant).

DOL (Degree of Operating Leverage) is: DOL = Contribution Margin ÷ Operating Income. Near breakeven, operating income is close to $0, so DOL becomes extremely large or undefined.

Indirect costs (overhead) are pooled and allocated to cost objects using an allocation base (e.g., DLH, machine hours, revenue).

Overhead rate is: OH Rate = Total Overhead Pool ÷ Total Allocation Base.

Death spiral intuition: if you drop a cost object, the remaining objects may have to absorb the same overhead pool, increasing the overhead rate and making more objects look unprofitable.

Manipulation / Data Quality Checks

  • Outliers: one unusual period can distort High-Low and regression.
  • Step costs: costs that jump at thresholds won’t fit a single straight line well.
  • Relevant range: the linear model is an approximation within a normal activity band.

Unit cost (average cost) includes fixed costs spread over units. If volume changes, unit cost changes.

The trap is using a unit cost computed at one volume and multiplying by a different volume to estimate total cost.

Always reason in total dollars: Total Cost = FC + (VC/unit × Units).

Common traps (MBA interview prep)

  • Using average cost to forecast totals when volume changes.
  • Assuming fixed costs “disappear” when volume drops (short run vs long run).
  • Ignoring step costs and capacity constraints.
