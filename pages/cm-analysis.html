📊 Contribution Margin Analysis

Analyze contribution margin, gross profit, breakeven, and optimal product mix under capacity constraints.

Contribution Margin (CM) is the amount left over after subtracting variable costs from revenue. It contributes first to covering fixed costs, then to profit.

Gross Profit (GP) subtracts only COGS (cost of goods sold) from revenue. The difference: CM uses all variable costs; GP uses only production-related costs you designate as COGS.

Breakeven is the point where total revenue equals total costs — operating income is exactly $0. Below breakeven you lose money; above it you profit.

Margin of Safety is how far your actual (or target) volume is above breakeven — expressed as a percentage. Higher is safer.

Known Volume mode — enter how many units you sold or plan to sell. The tool calculates actual operating income and shows where you stand relative to breakeven.

Target Profit mode — enter a profit goal (or $0 for breakeven). The tool solves for how many units you need to sell to hit that target.

Variable Costs (per unit)

Variable costs change in direct proportion to the number of units produced or sold. Common examples:

  • Direct Materials — raw materials used to make each unit
  • Direct Labor — wages paid per unit produced
  • Variable Manufacturing Overhead — utilities, supplies that vary with production
  • Variable Selling Costs — commissions, shipping per unit sold

Check COGS for costs that are part of the cost of goods sold (production costs). Leave unchecked for variable selling/admin costs. This determines Gross Profit vs Contribution Margin.

Add each variable cost line item. Check "COGS" if it should be included in Gross Profit calculation.

Fixed Costs (total per period)

Fixed costs remain constant regardless of how many units you produce or sell. Common examples:

  • Rent / Lease — facility costs that don't change with volume
  • Salaries — management and administrative staff
  • Depreciation — equipment and asset depreciation
  • Insurance — fixed premiums
  • Fixed Overhead — any production overhead that doesn't vary with output

Fixed costs are what the contribution margin must first cover before any profit is generated. The breakeven point is where CM exactly equals total fixed costs.

Breakeven Analysis

Sales Mix is the proportion of each product in total sales. If you sell 600 units of Product A and 400 of Product B, the mix is 60%/40%. Mix percentages must sum to 100%.

Weighted Average CM blends each product's CM per unit by its sales mix. This single number represents the average contribution of one "bundle" unit sold.

Bundle Breakeven is the total number of units (across all products) needed to cover fixed costs, assuming the sales mix holds constant.

Breakeven Option A allocates fixed costs to each product by its mix percentage, then solves for that product's individual units. Each product "pays its share" of fixed costs.

Breakeven Option B solves for total bundle units first, then multiplies by each product's mix. This is the standard managerial accounting approach.

Capacity Constraint — when a shared resource (machine hours, labor hours) is limited, you should prioritize products with the highest CM per unit of that resource — not just the highest CM per unit. This is the Theory of Constraints.

What-If Analysis

Sensitivity analysis shows how your results change when key assumptions change. This is critical for decision-making under uncertainty.

Price sensitivity — a small price increase often has a large impact on CM because variable costs don't change. This is called operating leverage.

Volume sensitivity — shows how much volume needs to change to hit breakeven or a profit target. Useful for sales planning.

Variable cost sensitivity — models the impact of input cost changes (materials, labor). If your supplier raises prices, how does that affect your breakeven?

Fixed cost sensitivity — models the impact of adding capacity, hiring staff, or signing a new lease.

The sensitivity matrix shows operating income at every combination of price and volume adjustments simultaneously — giving you a full picture of the risk/reward landscape.
